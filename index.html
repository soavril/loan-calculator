Common Questions

Why does the fixed rate loan look more expensive at first?

Fixed rates are typically set higher than initial variable rates because lenders price in the cost of rate stability. You're paying for predictability. Variable rates start lower but carry the risk of increasing over time.

When could a variable rate end up costing more?

If interest rates rise significantly during your loan term, a variable rate loan could become more expensive than a fixed rate option. This calculator shows a snapshot at current rates—actual costs depend on future rate movements.

Why might my bank's numbers be different from this calculator?

Banks may include fees, insurance, or use different calculation methods (daily vs monthly compounding). This calculator uses standard amortization formulas for comparison purposes. Always confirm final numbers with your lender.

Can I trust these calculation results?

This calculator uses standard financial formulas that match most loan calculators. However, it's a simplified model for comparison purposes. For actual loan decisions, verify numbers with your bank or financial institution.

What is amortization?

Amortization means your monthly payment stays the same, but the portion going to interest vs principal changes over time. Early payments are mostly interest; later payments are mostly principal.

Should I choose fixed or variable rate?

This calculator doesn't make recommendations. Your choice depends on your risk tolerance, financial situation, and expectations about future interest rates. We show you the numbers—you decide what's right for you.
